## Overview

Functional CPA (funcCPA) security, introduced by Akavia, Gentry, Halevi, and Vald, extends the standard CPA game with a *functional re-encryption oracle* that models settings where a server performing FHE computations is allowed to interactively delegate part of the computation back to the client (who holds the secret key).
In the funcCPA game, the adversary can submit a ciphertext $c$ and a function $g$ to a recryption oracle, which decrypts $c$, applies $g$, and returns a fresh encryption of the result.

The motivation comes from practical FHE deployments where bootstrapping - the most expensive operation - can be replaced by a cheaper interactive protocol: the server sends a masked ciphertext to the client (or a local trusted proxy such as a secure enclave), which decrypts and re-encrypts it, and the server removes the mask homomorphically.
The funcCPA notion captures the security requirement in this setting: even with access to this re-encryption oracle, the adversary should not be able to break indistinguishability.

The key result is that funcCPA is strictly stronger than CPA but strictly weaker than CCA1.
Intuitively, the re-encryption oracle is weaker than a full decryption oracle because it only returns encryptions of function outputs, never raw plaintexts.
Akavia et al. provided construction blueprints to turn CPA-secure FHE schemes into funcCPA-secure ones.

## Formal Definition

Given a public-key encryption scheme $\mathcal{E} = (\mathsf{KeyGen}, \mathsf{Enc}, \mathsf{Dec})$ and a family of functions $\mathcal{G}$, the funcCPA game proceeds as follows:

1. The challenger generates $(pk, sk) \leftarrow \mathsf{KeyGen}(1^\lambda)$ and gives $pk$ to $\mathcal{A}$.
2. $\mathcal{A}$ has access to a recryption oracle $\mathsf{RecryptO}$ which, on input $(g, c)$ with $g \in \mathcal{G}$ and $c \in \mathcal{C}$, returns $\mathsf{Enc}(g(\mathsf{Dec}(c)))$.
3. $\mathcal{A}$ outputs two messages $(m_0, m_1)$.
4. The challenger samples $b \xleftarrow{\textdollar} \{0,1\}$, computes $c^* \leftarrow \mathsf{Enc}(m_b)$, and sends $c^*$ to $\mathcal{A}$.
5. $\mathcal{A}$ retains access to $\mathsf{RecryptO}$.
6. $\mathcal{A}$ outputs a guess $b'$.

The scheme is funcCPA-secure if the advantage $|\Pr[b' = b] - 1/2|$ is negligible for all PPT adversaries.
A multi-input variant, funcCPA$^+$, where the oracle accepts multiple ciphertexts and a multi-variate function, has been shown equivalent to the baseline notion by Shinozaki, Tanaka, Tezuka, and Yoshida (ePrint 2024/1166).

## Attacks & Relevance

FuncCPA security is relevant in any deployment where the adversary can interact with an entity that performs decrypt-then-re-encrypt operations.
The canonical scenario is an FHE server that outsources bootstrapping to the client or to a secure enclave holding the secret key. Even though the enclave only sees masked values (not raw plaintexts), the funcCPA model captures the adversary's ability to adaptively choose which ciphertexts get refreshed and through which functions.

The strict separation from CPA means that some CPA-secure schemes become insecure when the adversary gains re-encryption access.
Conversely, the fact that CCA1 implies funcCPA (proven as part of Manulis and Nguyen's Theorem 4 in their Eurocrypt 2024 paper) means that any CCA1-secure scheme is automatically safe in this setting.

Fontaine, Renard, Sirdey, and Stan (ePrint 2025/2036) further showed that funcCPA-style extensions of CCA1 and vCCA (denoted CCA1R, CCA1M, vCCAR, vCCAM) all collapse back to their base notions.
This means that the gap between CPA and funcCPA does not propagate to stronger notions: once a scheme is CCA1 or vCCA secure, adding recryption or multiplication oracles does not weaken it.

## Achieving This Notion

Akavia, Gentry, Halevi, and Vald provided blueprints for constructing funcCPA-secure schemes from CPA-secure FHE.
Any CCA1-secure scheme trivially achieves funcCPA.
For concrete constructions, Fontaine et al. showed that the Paillier scheme (under the heuristic that it is CCA1) combined with a multiplication oracle implemented via a semi-honest secure enclave achieves CCA1M security, which implies funcCPA.
The "two-ciphertexts" construction $P^{(2)}$ based on Paillier, which achieves vCCA under a CPA + Linear-Only Homomorphism assumption, also achieves funcCPA as vCCA implies CCA1 which implies funcCPA.

## Further Reading

The funcCPA notion was introduced by Akavia, Gentry, Halevi, and Vald (TCC 2022; Journal of Cryptology 2025).
The equivalence between funcCPA and funcCPA$^+$ was established by Shinozaki, Tanaka, Tezuka, and Yoshida (ePrint 2024/1166).
Dodis, Halevi, and Wichs (TCC 2023) further studied the setting where delegation targets a local client proxy.
For the collapse of funcCPA-style CCA variants onto their base notions, see Fontaine, Renard, Sirdey, and Stan (ePrint 2025/2036).
